Imagine you are on a highway, heading to a place you’ve never been. You have no map and no GPS signal, so your only way to get there is by following the road signs. After a while, you realize you haven’t seen a single sign: no directions to a town, no speed limits, no junctions… even the lane lines aren't marked. Suddenly, you see a car heading toward you at full speed in the wrong direction. Panic takes over your body as you brace for the inevitable.

A road without signs is just like a relationship without boundaries: No one knows what is allowed and what isn't; no one knows if they are respecting the other's space. In such a scenario, an accident is highly likely. Just as traffic signs help us drive safely, boundaries in relationships serve the same function: they ensure that bonds are healthy and safe to protect the integrity of both people. We have the right to set boundaries, but not just any way—we must do so while considering both the other person's emotions and our own. This is key to ensuring these limits are healthy and help us build, rather than destroy.

To start, Alba Cardala, a clinical psychologist and neuropsychologist, defines boundaries as "a real or symbolic line that marks the end of something, whether material or non-material." Specifically, in psychology, we speak of interpersonal boundaries, which can be defined as rules or lines we establish and must follow in our relationships to ensure we don't hurt anyone.

Types of Boundaries

• Physical Boundaries:
These refer to personal space and physical contact. Some people are comfortable with physical touch and interact through hugs or kisses on the cheek, while others prefer to keep their distance—perhaps only waving hello—and feel uncomfortable when someone gets too close. These boundaries also depend on context, such as a country's culture (for example, Asian countries tend to be more distant when greeting compared to Latin American countries) or the person you are with. For obvious reasons, you wouldn't greet a teacher the same way you greet your mother.

• Personal Space Boundaries:
These involve our private spaces, like our home or our bed. These boundaries are broken when someone invades our private areas or snoops through our things without permission.

• Emotional Boundaries:
These relate to how we express our emotions. These boundaries are violated when someone invalidates or judges our feelings, or when someone manipulates us emotionally. This includes shifting blame for things that aren't your fault, gaslighting (distorting reality), or making you believe you are incapable so that you need their approval, creating a dependency. It is also a violation of emotional boundaries when someone eavesdrops on your conversations, reads your messages, or searches for places where you may have left private thoughts or emotions.

• Time Boundaries:
These refer to how we manage our time for different activities and people in our daily lives. These are violated when someone forces us to spend more time with them than we want, or consistently shows up late to things that were agreed upon for a specific time.

• Material Boundaries:
These concern private property and material goods—how we use them and who we choose to share them with. These boundaries are broken when someone takes something without permission, steals it, breaks it without replacing it, or forces us to use something.

How to Set a Healthy Boundary

It is important to remember that boundaries are not about demanding or forbidding someone from doing something; boundaries are about expressing how we feel regarding something and communicating our needs within the relationship.

Having the right to state what we need does not mean we can do it in any manner without considering the other person's feelings. A boundary is not saying, "I want you to text me every day when you get home." Instead, it is saying, "I would feel comfortable if you let me know when you got home." This gives the other person the choice to participate.

When we understand what a boundary is (and what it isn't), we begin to see them as a tool that helps us improve our relationships. Through them, we preserve both our own dignity and the dignity of others.
